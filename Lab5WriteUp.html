Modeling Monatomic and Diatomic Wires

Abstract

        The resonant frequencies of a ball and spring model can be calculated using different types of expressions and models. In this lab, the resonant frequencies of a monatomic wire, a diatomic wire, and a monatomic wire with an oddball were calculated using an analytic expression and a matrix eigenvalue equation. For the diatomic wire, when comparing data collected against the expected values, the analytical expression had a reduced chi square of 3.47 whereas the matrix eigenvalue equation that does not account for the mass of the wire had a reduced chi square of 0.513 and the matrix eigenvalue equation that does account for the mass of the wire had a reduced chi square of 1.27. For the monatomic wire with odd mass, when comparing data collected against the expected values, the matrix eigenvalue equation that does not account for the mass of the wire had a reduced chi square of 0.498 and the matrix eigenvalue equation that does account for the mass of the wire had a reduced chi square of 0.867. The matrix eigenvalue equation was more accurate compared to the analytical expression since it was able to calculate higher frequencies with shorter wavelengths.  

Introduction

        A ball and spring model is widely used in physics to help understand the useful resonances of strings and diaphragms and in some cases the specific heat capacity of solids. This lab examines a real macroscopic ball and spring model in order to experiment with resonant frequencies. More specifically a monatomic wire and a diatomic wire are looked at in a few different circumstances and compared to determine how the resonant frequencies are affected. Monatomic wires have just one type of ball while diatomic wires have alternating balls. It is interesting how the results differ once the balls on the diatomic wire are altered and when the mass of the wire itself is taken into consideration. In order to find these results an RCA stereo amplifier is used to pass output to a PASCO Driver which in turn will vibrate the wire and the PASCO detector picks up these resonant frequencies.

Procedure

        The equipment used in these experiments consisted of a lock-in amplifier, an RCA stereo amplifier, a PASCO driver, detector and track, a steel string, spherical lead weights, hanging masses, and a computer with a downloaded LabView program. The setup, as shown in Figure 2, involved clamping the PASCO track to a table to avoid tipping then securing a steel wire to one end of the track and hanging masses to the other end. The PASCO detector is attached to the input of the lock-in amplifier then the RCA stereo amplifier is attached to the output of the lock-in amplifier. The output of the amplifier is then attached to the PASCO driver which sits underneath one end of the steel wire. The PASCO works by using electromagnetism in order to alternate between attracting and repelling the wire which in turn drives the oscillation. The PASCO detector is placed underneath the other end of the steel wire and is sending output back to the lock-in amplifier. When data was collected the frequency range was set from 0 to 110 Hertz with a time step size of 5000 and a delay of 2.5 s.

Figure 2: Diagram of the experimental setup involving a PASCO track, driver, and detector along with a ball and string model connected to hanging masses.

        Monatomic Wire

        

        The monatomic wire is set up by placing ten equal spherical masses spaced evenly across the steel wire. The mass of each ball was about 2.48 g. Bridges were set up on either side of the track just outside of the driver and detector so they were 2 meters apart. The masses were then spaced 0.2 meters apart between the bridges. The data that was taken was the amplitude of the wire’s motion and the driving frequencies.

        Diatomic Wire

        The diatomic wire is then set up by placing ten spherical masses spaced evenly across the steel wire, however the masses alternate between a small mass and a big mass. The small mass was about 1.66 g and the big mass was about 3.356 g. The data for the amplitude of the wire’s motion and the driving frequencies were collected.

        

        Monatomic Wire with Different Mass

        A monatomic wire was then set up exactly how it was in the first part of the experiment however one of the masses on the string was switched with a smaller mass so the wire had nine equal masses and one different. The odd mass was placed at the fourth spot from the detector or the seventh from the driver. The nine equal masses were about 2.2789 g and the odd mass was 1.66 g. The data was taken for the amplitudes of the wire’s motion and the driving frequencies.

Conclusion

        The resonant frequencies were determined for a monatomic wire, a diatomic wire, and a monatomic wire with an odd mass. For the monatomic wire the fitting parameter was estimated to be 66.1  0.2 Hz which is consistent and within the uncertainty of the expected parameter of 65.1  3.9 Hz. At larger frequencies, the expected curve becomes an underestimate because it does not account for the mass of the string.  For the diatomic wire, the fitting curve estimated the fitting parameter to be A = 69.208  0.786 Hz and B = 0.938  0.0155. The accurate curve estimated the fitting parameter to be A = 68.652  0.896 Hz and B = 0.896  0.004. With both of these curves the A parameters were consistent and within the uncertainties of the expected value of A = 68.754  0.896 Hz, however, the B parameters were not consistent and fell close to 10% of the expected value of B = 0.885  0.004 Hz. When comparing data collected for the diatomic wire against the expected values, the analytical expression had a reduced chi square of 3.47 whereas the matrix eigenvalue equation that does not account for the mass of the wire had a reduced chi square of 0.513 and the matrix eigenvalue equation that does account for the mass of the wire had a reduced chi square of 1.27. It was determined that at higher frequencies the analytical expression could not correctly determine the resonant frequencies because the wavelengths were too small. However, the matrix eigenvalue equation is able to calculate those larger frequencies. For the monatomic wire with an odd mass, it is expected that the odd mass affects the resonant frequencies that have nearby antinodes to it. When comparing data collected against the expected values, the matrix eigenvalue equation that does not account for the mass of the wire had a reduced chi square of 0.498 and the matrix eigenvalue equation that does account for the mass of the wire had a reduced chi square of 0.867. Overall, it was determined that using the matrix eigenvector equation is more accurate compared to the analytical expression since it can calculate the higher frequencies with lower wavelengths. The matrix also allows us to account for the mass of the wire, but future calculations need to account for the tension of the wire.
